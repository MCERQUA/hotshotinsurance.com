# Non-CDL Hotshot Insurance: Requirements for Under-26k Operations

One of the most common questions we get: **"Can I run hotshot without a CDL?"** Yes — many hotshot owner-operators run non-CDL with a Class 3–5 pickup (an F-350/450/550, a Ram, or a GM HD) and a gooseneck, pintle, or flatbed trailer, staying under a 26,000 lb combined GVWR. But "no CDL" does **not** mean "no insurance requirements." If anything, the insurance stack is what keeps a non-CDL operation legal and profitable.

Here's what every non-CDL hotshot operator needs to understand.

## You Still Need MC/USDOT Authority for Interstate For-Hire

If you haul for-hire across state lines, you generally need your own **MC/USDOT authority** — regardless of CDL status. That authority comes with a hard insurance requirement: FMCSA requires **primary liability** (commonly $750,000, almost always written at $1,000,000) plus **cargo coverage** filed on the proper forms before your authority goes active.

A non-CDL truck doesn't exempt you from FMCSA minimums. The authority — not the license — is what triggers the filing requirements.

## The Non-CDL Insurance Stack

A properly structured non-CDL hotshot program typically includes:

- **Primary trucking liability.** The FMCSA-required coverage (commonly $1M) that protects the public when you're at fault.
- **Motor truck cargo.** Covers the freight — most brokers require $100K minimum, and oilfield and high-value loads want more.
- **Physical damage.** Collision and comprehensive on your truck and trailer — liability only pays the other guy.
- **Bobtail / non-trucking liability.** Covers you deadheading, bobtailing, or on personal miles when primary doesn't respond.
- **Occupational accident.** The owner-operator's alternative to workers' comp — required by most carrier lease agreements.
- **General liability.** Covers off-roadway exposure (loading, unloading, shipper lots) — increasingly required by brokers.

## Why Non-CDL Doesn't Mean Cheaper-Than-CDL

Don't assume a non-CDL operation is automatically the cheaper route. While CDL status can lower pricing with some markets, the cost of your program is driven far more by **authority, cargo limits, radius, and truck value** than by the license in your wallet. A non-CDL operator running their own MC authority with $100K cargo and a long-haul radius can pay as much as — or more than — a CDL driver.

The bigger cost question is **leased vs. independent authority**, not CDL vs. non-CDL.

## Stay Under 26,000 lbs Combined GVWR

The non-CDL threshold hinges on **combined** gross vehicle weight rating (GCWR) — truck plus trailer — staying under 26,000 lbs. Exceed that, and a Class A CDL typically becomes required for the combination. If you're running non-CDL deliberately, know your numbers and don't accidentally cross the line with an oversize gooseneck the DOT reclassifies.

## What Brokers and Load Boards Actually Require

Even where FMCSA doesn't strictly mandate a line, the market does. Most brokers, load boards, and oilfield accounts require proof of:

- $1M primary liability
- $100K cargo (often more)
- General liability (increasingly)
- Listed as certificate holder or additional insured

A non-CDL operator without these can't get the best loads — coverage, not the license, is what unlocks the freight.
