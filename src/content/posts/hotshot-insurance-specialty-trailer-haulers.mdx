# Hotshot Insurance for Specialty Trailer Haulers: Campers, Oversized Loads & Non-Standard Freight

The hotshot trucking community has evolved far beyond oilfield pipe runs and building materials. Scroll through any owner-operator Facebook group today and you'll find operators hauling **Freedom Campers on PJ flatbeds, oversize truck camper units, pop-up trailers, skid steers, specialty manufactured products** — loads that don't fit the standard cargo insurance mold.

That evolution creates an insurance gap that trips up a lot of good operators. The standard motor truck cargo policy that covers pipe or lumber may not cover a $45,000 camper unit the same way — and the specialty nature of the freight changes the liability exposure too.

Here's what actually changes when you specialize in non-standard freight, and how to build a program that keeps you covered.

## What Makes Specialty Trailer Hauling Different from Standard Hotshot

Standard hotshot cargo — oilfield equipment, building materials, steel, agricultural equipment — is relatively predictable from an insurance standpoint. Carriers understand the exposure, rates are established, and most motor truck cargo policies cover it without endorsements.

**Specialty trailer hauling introduces new variables:**

- **High per-unit values.** A Freedom Camper unit or oversize truck camper can run $35,000–$80,000+. That's a cargo exposure most standard hotshot policies weren't written for.
- **Unconventional loading.** Specialty units often require chains, straps, and blocking arrangements that differ from pipe or lumber securing. A cargo claim involving improper securement becomes a liability argument — not just a cargo claim.
- **Manufacturer relationships.** Many specialty haulers work directly for manufacturers like Freedom Campers or similar brands. Those manufacturers have insurance requirements built into their carrier contracts — often higher cargo limits than a standard hotshot program carries.
- **Oversize permit exposure.** Larger camper units and specialty manufactured goods may require [oversize/overweight permits](https://ops.fhwa.dot.gov/freight/sw/permit_report/index.htm) in multiple states. Operating oversize without proper permits can void a cargo claim.
- **Delivery point liability.** Delivering a high-value unit to a dealer or customer creates premises liability exposure at the delivery point that a simple pipe delivery doesn't.

Understanding these differences is how you build a program that actually pays at claim time — not one that looks complete on a certificate but falls apart when you need it.

## The Core Coverages (and How They Apply to Specialty Loads)

### Motor Truck Cargo Insurance

Motor truck cargo (MTC) is the foundation. It covers the freight you're hauling — but **the limit matters enormously** when you're moving specialty units.

Standard hotshot cargo policies often carry **$100,000 limits** — adequate for pipe or building materials, but potentially thin for a load of two premium camper units at $45,000 each.

**What to watch for with specialty loads:**

- **Per-occurrence vs. per-unit limits.** Some MTC policies have per-unit sublimits that cap what they pay per individual item. A $100K policy with a $25K-per-unit sublimit is a problem when you're hauling three units worth $30K each.
- **Excluded commodities.** Review your policy for commodity exclusions. Some MTC policies exclude **recreational vehicles, manufactured housing, and specialty units** by category — the exact freight you're hauling. This exclusion must be removed via endorsement or you need a different policy.
- **Theft coverage.** High-value camper units are attractive theft targets, especially during overnight stops. Confirm your policy covers theft of the freight, not just collision and overturn damage.
- **Loading and unloading coverage.** MTC can be written to include damage during loading/unloading — critical when you're strapping down an $80,000 unit with chains and a come-along.

**Recommended minimum for specialty haulers: $150,000–$250,000 MTC**, with no per-unit sublimits and no recreational vehicle exclusions.

### Primary Trucking Liability

[Primary trucking liability](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/registration/commercial-drivers-license/minimum-insurance-requirements) is the FMCSA-required coverage for operators running under their own MC authority — minimum $750,000 for most freight, though many brokers and manufacturers require **$1,000,000** for specialty and high-value loads.

For specialty trailer haulers, primary liability matters in a way it doesn't for pipe runs: if you're delivering a $60,000 camper unit to a dealer lot and a wheel chock slips during unloading, causing the unit to roll and damage property — that's a liability claim, not a cargo claim. Make sure your primary liability is adequate for the delivery scenarios you encounter.

### Physical Damage — Truck and Trailer

Your **PJ Trailers flatbed or gooseneck** represents a significant investment — the Voyager series goosenecks run $25,000–$45,000+ new. Physical damage (collision + comprehensive) covers your trailer when it's damaged in an accident, regardless of fault.

**Key points for specialty haulers:**

- Make sure your stated trailer value reflects **actual replacement cost**, not what you paid three years ago. Trailer values have risen sharply since 2021.
- If you run multiple trailers for different load types, each should be on the policy at its correct value.
- **Downtime loss** after a trailer is damaged is real — factor in how long you'd be off the road waiting for repairs or replacement when choosing your coverage.

### Bobtail / Non-Trucking Liability

[Non-trucking liability (NTL)](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/safety/driver-safety/cmv-driving-tips-non-trucking-liability) covers you when you're operating your truck for personal use — not under dispatch, not hauling freight. This matters for specialty haulers who dead-head between loads or use their work truck for personal errands.

If you're leased to a carrier, **bobtail** (not under dispatch, no trailer attached) is the version of this you likely need. Independent owner-operators typically need NTL rather than bobtail, but the distinction matters — don't assume one is the same as the other.

### Occupational Accident Insurance

Hotshot trucking doesn't come with workers' compensation unless you're a W-2 employee. **[Occupational accident insurance](https://www.iihs.org/topics/fatality-statistics/detail/large-trucks)** is the owner-operator's substitute — it covers medical expenses, disability benefits, and accidental death if you're injured on the job.

For specialty haulers, the injury exposure is real: securing heavy camper units, climbing on flatbeds, managing chain and strap loads during loading and delivery creates physical exposure that a standard drive-a-pallet-jack cargo run doesn't. Occ-acc should be on every specialty hauler's program.

### General Liability

General liability covers third-party bodily injury and property damage claims that aren't covered by your trucking liability — specifically, claims that arise from your business operations but not from the truck itself.

For specialty haulers, GL becomes relevant when:
- You work directly with a manufacturer and the contract requires GL
- You're doing business at dealer lots or customer properties during delivery
- You operate a home office or storage yard

Many specialty freight contracts — especially manufacturer-direct arrangements — **require $1,000,000 GL as a condition of doing business**. Don't wait for a contract dispute to discover you don't carry it.

## Oversize and Overweight Loads: The Permit Coverage Gap

One of the most common coverage gaps for specialty haulers involves **oversize/overweight (OS/OW) loads**.

A large truck camper, Freedom Camper-style unit, or specialty manufactured product can easily exceed standard width (8'6") or height (13'6") limits that define legal loads in most states. Once you cross those thresholds, you need:

- **State OS/OW permits** — each state the load travels through
- **Pilot/escort cars** in some configurations
- **Route surveys** for low bridges and weight-restricted roads

**The insurance angle:** some MTC policies contain language that voids cargo coverage if a load is transported in violation of state permit requirements. If you're hauling an oversized unit without the required permits and it's damaged in transit, the carrier may deny the cargo claim on the grounds that you were operating illegally.

**Best practice:** obtain all required permits before the load moves, and work with an insurance agent who understands the OS/OW space. The [Federal Highway Administration's permit database](https://ops.fhwa.dot.gov/freight/sw/permit_report/index.htm) is a resource for understanding state-by-state requirements.

## What Specialty Freight Contracts Require

If you're hauling for a manufacturer directly — Freedom Campers, a camper manufacturer, a specialty vehicle builder — their carrier contracts typically specify insurance requirements. Common requirements we see for specialty freight:

| Coverage | Typical Minimum Required |
|---|---|
| Primary Trucking Liability | $1,000,000 per occurrence |
| Motor Truck Cargo | $100,000–$250,000 (varies by unit value) |
| General Liability | $1,000,000 per occurrence |
| Physical Damage | Full actual value of truck + trailer |
| Occupational Accident | Often recommended, sometimes required |

If your current program doesn't meet these thresholds, you won't qualify for the better specialty freight contracts — regardless of how good an operator you are.

Specialty trailer hauler programs vary significantly based on the specifics of the operation, but here's a general framework:

- **Primary trucking liability ($1M):** $4,000–$9,000/year for a clean record, varies by radius and state filings
- **Motor truck cargo ($150K–$250K, no RV exclusion):** $1,200–$3,500/year depending on commodity and limits

## Building the Right Program

The mistake most specialty haulers make is either:

1. **Buying a generic hotshot program** and hoping the RV/specialty exclusions don't apply
2. **Underinsuring the cargo** at $100K when the loads they run consistently exceed that

Neither works when you have a claim.

A properly built specialty trailer hauler program:

- Removes commodity exclusions that would apply to your freight
- Carries cargo limits that actually cover the value of your highest-value loads
- Includes GL if your contracts require it
- Has physical damage on your trailer at current replacement cost
- Includes occ-acc so you're covered if you get hurt on a delivery

Getting there starts with a conversation — not a one-size-fits-all online quote. Specialty freight exposure requires a real carrier market, not a standard personal lines auto carrier with a commercial endorsement.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does my hotshot cargo policy cover campers and recreational vehicles?**

Not automatically. Many standard motor truck cargo policies contain commodity exclusions for recreational vehicles, manufactured housing, and specialty units. You need an MTC policy that specifically includes these commodities — either on the base policy or via endorsement. Ask to see the commodity schedule before you accept coverage.

**What cargo limit do I need to haul high-value specialty units?**

At minimum, your cargo limit should equal the value of the highest-value full load you'd carry at one time. If you can haul two $45,000 camper units simultaneously, you want at least $90,000–$100,000 in cargo coverage — and $150,000–$250,000 is more appropriate given that cargo values often exceed invoice price at claim settlement.

**Do I need oversize permits for camper units?**

Many specialty truck camper units and manufactured products exceed standard width (8'6") or height (13'6") limits and require OS/OW permits. Requirements vary by state and load configuration. Operating oversize without proper permits can void your cargo coverage — check state requirements for every state the load passes through.

**Can I run specialty freight without my own MC authority?**

Yes — you can operate under a carrier's authority as a leased owner-operator. However, under that arrangement, the carrier's cargo policy covers the freight, not yours. If you're running under your own MC authority, you need your own MTC policy with appropriate limits and no problematic exclusions.

**What does "per-unit sublimit" mean and why does it matter?**

A per-unit sublimit is a cap within your cargo policy that limits what the policy pays per individual item, even if your total cargo limit is higher. Example: a $150K policy with a $30K per-unit sublimit would only pay $30K on a $55,000 camper unit that's a total loss — leaving you $25K short. Confirm your policy has no per-unit sublimits, or that the sublimits exceed the value of individual items you haul.

**How do I find an agency that covers specialty freight?**

Not all commercial insurance agencies have access to specialty trucking markets. Look for an independent agency with dedicated trucking experience and relationships with A-rated trucking carriers. Ask specifically whether they can write motor truck cargo for recreational vehicles or specialty manufactured products — and ask to see the commodity endorsement before you bind.
