# What Does Motor Truck Cargo Insurance Actually Cover?

Primary liability covers the public. Physical damage covers your rig. **Motor truck cargo** covers the load — the freight in your care, custody, and control. For a hotshot operator, it's the line that pays the shipper or broker when the freight doesn't arrive in the condition it was tendered. And almost every broker, shipper, and load board requires it before they'll hand you a load.

Here's what cargo actually covers, what it doesn't, and how to size it right.

## What Motor Truck Cargo Covers

Cargo insurance protects the freight while it's in your care — typically from pickup to delivery. Covered perils generally include:

- **Damage to the freight** from a crash, sudden stop, shifting load, or rough handling
- **Loss of the freight** — a load that's destroyed or rendered unusable
- **Theft of cargo** from the trailer, the yard, or in transit
- **Reefer breakdown and contamination** (when the option is carried) for temperature-sensitive freight
- **Loading and unloading exposure** depending on the form

The key concept is **care, custody, and control** — if the freight is your responsibility when it's damaged, lost, or stolen, cargo is the line that responds.

## What Cargo Insurance Does NOT Cover

Cargo has clear boundaries. It is **not**:

- **Your truck or trailer** — that's physical damage
- **Third-party bodily injury or property damage in a wreck** — that's primary liability
- **Your own injuries** — that's occupational accident or workers' comp
- **Non-owned trailers you pull** — that's trailer interchange

Operators sometimes assume "if anything happens to the load, I'm covered." Cargo covers the freight itself — not the equipment or the liability, which are separate lines that all need to be in place.

## How Much Cargo Insurance Do You Need?

This is the question that decides whether you get the loads you want. The market — not the law — sets the floor:

- **$100,000** is the de facto minimum most brokers and load boards require.
- **$150,000–$300,000+** is common for oilfield pipe and equipment, machinery, steel, and high-value freight.
- **Specific shipper contracts** (especially Permian, Bakken, and Gulf Coast accounts) can demand defined minimums above the standard.

Size cargo to the freight you actually haul and the shippers you want to work with. A default number that's too low gets you turned down for the best-paying loads — and there's no faster way to cap your income than an undersized cargo limit.

## FMCSA Filings and Your Authority

If you operate interstate under your own authority, FMCSA requires cargo coverage filed on the proper form to keep your MC authority active. A lapsed cargo filing can suspend your authority and put you out of service. We handle the filings and monitor them so a billing issue never takes you off the road.

## Coordinating Cargo With the Rest of the Stack

Cargo doesn't operate in isolation. A loaded-trailer wreck can trigger liability (the other party), cargo (the freight), and physical damage (your rig) simultaneously. We coordinate all three so there's no gap where a claim falls between policies — which is exactly where most coverage disputes happen.
