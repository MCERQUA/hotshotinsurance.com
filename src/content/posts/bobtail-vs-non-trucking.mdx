# Bobtail vs. Non-Trucking Liability: What's the Difference?

**Bobtail insurance** and **non-trucking liability (NTL)** are two of the most confused — and most important — lines in a hotshot program. They sound similar, they fill related gaps, and getting them wrong can leave you with an uncovered claim during the exact miles you assumed you were protected.

Here's the plain-English difference, and why it matters for your operation.

## The Gap They Both Fill

First, understand the gap. **Primary trucking liability** covers you while you're operating under a load or in dispatched, revenue service. The moment you're off-load — deadheading to the next pickup, bobtailing home after a delivery, or running a personal errand in the truck — primary liability may not respond.

That's the deadhead-and-bobtail gap, and it's where most off-load claims become coverage disasters. **Bobtail and non-trucking liability both exist to fill that gap.** But they fill it differently.

## Bobtail Liability

Bobtail covers you driving the **power unit without a trailer attached** — "bobtailing." The classic scenario: you deliver a load, drop the trailer, and bobtail the truck to the next pickup, to a fuel stop, or back to the yard.

Bobtail is most commonly associated with **leased owner-operators**. Most motor-carrier lease agreements require the leased operator to carry bobtail liability for those no-trailer miles, because the carrier's primary liability is built around dispatched, loaded operation.

## Non-Trucking Liability (NTL)

Non-trucking liability is **broader**. It covers personal, non-revenue use of the truck — whether or not a trailer is attached. The scenarios NTL addresses that bobtail alone may not:

- Driving the truck for **personal use** (errands, commuting, weekends)
- Operating **outside dispatched business**, even with a trailer
- Miles that are neither under a load nor strictly "bobtailing"

NTL is built around the concept of **non-business, non-revenue use** — the miles that exist outside your for-hire operation entirely.

## Which One Do You Need?

The right answer depends on your operation and your lease:

- **Leased owner-operator?** Your carrier's lease agreement typically specifies bobtail, NTL, or both. Match the lease exactly — the language in your contract dictates what you carry.
- **Independent with your own authority?** You may still want NTL for true personal miles that primary liability doesn't cover. We structure it based on how you actually use the truck off-duty.
- **Power-only or running a mix?** You may need both — NTL for personal miles and bobtail for no-trailer deadhead — depending on the agreements you operate under.

## Why Getting It Wrong Is Expensive

These lines are cheap relative to the catastrophe they prevent. A single off-load at-fault wreck — bobtailing home from a delivery, or a personal-mile fender-bender — can produce a liability claim that primary won't touch. Without the right coverage in place, that claim comes out of your pocket and your assets.

The cost of bobtail or NTL for a year is a fraction of what one uncovered off-load claim can cost.

## The Bottom Line

Bobtail covers the truck without a trailer; non-trucking covers personal and non-revenue use. Match the coverage to your lease and your actual off-load miles — and never assume primary liability follows you off a load.
